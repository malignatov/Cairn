# decision_principle_evaluations

How a committed decision relates to a ranked principle. This bridge table is
where the three-case model lives — the conceptual core of the principles
layer.

Every decision-principle relationship is one of three cases:

1. **`aligned`** — the decision honors the principle. No friction, logged
   quietly. Record only *meaningful* alignments, not every trivially
   compatible one — recording alignment for every principle on every
   decision is noise.
2. **`justified_override`** — the decision departs from principle X, but in
   service of a **higher-ranked** principle Y. This is the hierarchy
   functioning correctly, not a failure. Logged with rationale and a pointer
   to the higher principle. The scanner treats it as healthy and never
   alarms on it.
3. **`unjustified_departure`** — the decision departs from a principle with
   no higher-ranked principle invoked. This is the only case the scanner
   cares about, and even then only when it *repeats* (2+ for the same
   principle in 90 days). A single one is just an ordinary day.

Recorded by the `capture` skill, after the decision is committed, via
`state_record_principle_evaluation`. Only decisions are evaluated — not
projects, commitments, or other entities. (`opportunity_research` also
reasons about principles, but advisorily and forward-looking; it writes
nothing here.)

## Fields

- `id` — uuid, generated by the server.
- `decision_id` *(required)* — FK to the committed `decisions` row.
- `principle_id` *(required)* — FK to the principle in question (the one
  honored or departed from).
- `relationship` *(required)* — `aligned` | `justified_override` |
  `unjustified_departure`.
- `overrides_principle_id` — FK to the higher-ranked principle being served.
  Required when `relationship = justified_override`; null otherwise.
- `severity` — `low` | `medium` | `high`. Meaningful only for
  `unjustified_departure`, where it scales with the departed principle's
  rank (top third → high, middle → medium, bottom → low, by tertiles of the
  active count). Null for `aligned` and `justified_override`.
- `rationale` — required for `justified_override` and
  `unjustified_departure`; optional for `aligned`.
- `evaluated_at` — timestamp.

## Validation (enforced by storage)

- `justified_override` requires `overrides_principle_id` **and** `rationale`,
  and the override target must be **higher-ranked** (a smaller `rank` number)
  than the departed principle. If the named "higher" principle doesn't
  actually outrank the departed one, this isn't an override — it's just a
  departure, and the write is rejected.
- `unjustified_departure` requires `rationale`. `severity` is auto-derived
  from the departed principle's rank when not supplied.
- `aligned` clears `severity` and `overrides_principle_id` (they're
  meaningless there).

## Notes

- Departures and overrides are always recorded; alignment only where notable.
- The framing is deliberate throughout: "departed from," not "violated";
  "worth examining," not "you failed." Justified overrides are the system
  working. When a pattern of unjustified departures surfaces, it is
  genuinely open about which thing to revisit — the behavior or the
  principle. The user decides; the system surfaces.
